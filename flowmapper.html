Spatial Distribution:

Analyzing the spatial distribution of information upon the map offers many different aspects to focus on. One can look at individual variable distribution and then the combination of them all in the entire map. Beginning with focusing on the Net Dispersal of Family Heritage, which is displayed as a choropleth map, the most prevalent and extreme classifications are within the midwestern US, with the extreme negative classification for Iowa, Missouri, and Kansas, and the extreme positive distribution to be Oklahoma. Further, higher trends of negative classifications are upon the east coast, and positive upon the west. The higher extremes of both classifications are relatively clustered towards the middle of the US. Focusing of the nods, which represent the total events of dispersal, it shows three main extremes of high events, within Texas, Oklahoma, and Missouri. Specifically, Missouri and Oklahoma make sense due to the choropleth map, but Texas surprised me as it has a low, positive net dispersal classification. Florida, which the net dispersal was considered neutral, had the lowest total event classification, which makes intuitive sense. The flow patterns display the complexity of the system, but not to the point of confusion, but most extremely, high counts are mapped going both ways from Texas and Oklahoma. Viewing and analyzing the map as a whole, I am most drawn to the intensity of classifications, for all variables, clustered within the middle of the US. It provides insightful information revolving around the high rate of dispersal within those states in comparison to other states of the contagious US.
